# Grilling Your Bakery Expansion Plan

Okay, let's get into it. You've given me four pillars: a profitable track record, a hired manager, a replicated menu, and a loan-funded buildout. Each one deserves scrutiny.

---

## 1. "Profitable for 3 years" — but what does that actually mean?

- What are your actual margins? Bakeries are notoriously thin — 5-10% net is common. Are you profitable because of volume, a killer product, or just low rent at location one?
- How much of that profitability depends on YOU being there? If you're the one opening at 4am, managing staff relationships, and handling quality control personally, your "profitable business" might actually be a profitable owner-operator job — not a scalable model.
- Have you separated owner's compensation from profit in your numbers? A lot of small business owners confuse "I paid myself" with "the business is profitable."

---

## 2. "Hire a manager" — this is your biggest risk and you're glossing over it

- Do you have a candidate in mind, or is this theoretical? Finding someone you trust to run your business is extremely hard.
- What's your plan if the manager quits 3 months in, after you've already signed a lease and taken out a loan?
- Have you managed a manager before? Delegating is a skill. A lot of successful owner-operators are terrible at it when they try to scale.
- What does the manager compensation look like? A good manager who can actually run a location independently commands real money. Have you baked that into your financial projections?
- How will you hold them accountable for quality, culture, and financials without micromanaging from 20 minutes away?

---

## 3. "Replicate the same menu" — why would you assume that works?

- Is the new neighborhood the same demographic as your current one? A menu calibrated for one customer base may underperform for another.
- What's the competitive landscape at the new location? Are there other bakeries within walking distance? Coffee shops that sell pastries? A Whole Foods?
- "High foot traffic" is not a customer base. Who are those people? Office workers who want grab-and-go? Families on weekends? Students? Each requires a different product and pricing strategy.
- Have you done any validation — even informal — at the new location? Sold at a farmers market nearby? Asked anyone?

---

## 4. "Small business loan to cover the buildout" — define small, and define your assumptions

- What's the total buildout cost? Bakery build-outs are expensive: ventilation, ovens, refrigeration, permitting, build-out labor. $150k-$400k is not unusual. What's your number?
- What are the loan terms? Rate, repayment period, monthly payment? Have you stress-tested this against a slow first year?
- How long do you expect before location two is cash-flow positive? Restaurants and bakeries commonly take 12-18 months to break even. Can you service the debt during that period using only location one's profits?
- What happens to location one if the loan requires a personal guarantee and location two fails? Are you risking everything you built?

---

## 5. The question you haven't answered: why now?

- Is this expansion driven by opportunity (you found a great space, the timing is right, demand is exceeding capacity), or is it driven by ambition or restlessness?
- Those are very different motivations and they lead to very different outcomes.
- What does your first location look like operationally right now — is it running smoothly without you, or are you the load-bearing wall?

---

## Bottom line

Your plan has the shape of a good idea but not yet the substance of one. The profitable track record is real and valuable — don't discount it. But the two hardest parts of what you're describing (finding and retaining a great manager, and correctly sizing and financing the buildout) are being treated as solved problems when they're actually your biggest execution risks.

Before you sign anything, I'd want to see: a detailed pro forma with conservative revenue assumptions, a realistic manager compensation and contingency plan, and evidence that the new neighborhood actually wants what you're selling.
